Betty Holberton

Holberton invented breakpoints in computer debugging.1

A bit about her life:

  • Born Frances Elizabeth Snyder in Philadelphia, Pennsylvania in 1917.
  • Studied journalism, because its curriculum let her travel far afield.
  • During WW II, she was hired by the Moore School of Engineering to work as a "computer" and chosen to be one of the six women to program the ENIAC.
  • After World War II, Holberton worked at Remington Rand and the National Bureau of Standards.
  • She helped to develop the UNIVAC, designing control panels that put the numeric keypad next to the keyboard and persuading engineers to replace the Univac's black exterior with the gray-beige tone that came to be the universal color of computers.
  • She was one of those who wrote the first generative programming system (SORT/MERGE).
  • In 1953 she was made a supervisor of advanced programming in a part of the Navyâ€™s Applied Math lab in Maryland, where she stayed until 1966.
  • Later, as an employee of the National Bureau of Standards, she was very active in the first two revisions of the Fortran language standard ("FORTRAN 77" and "Fortran 90").
